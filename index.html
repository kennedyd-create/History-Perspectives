Know Your Target

Understand exactly what the examiners (and the Chief Marker) are looking for.

NZQA Chief Marker Advice

Choosing a specific, well-defined historical context is crucial. Candidates should focus on the "why" (motivations and beliefs) rather than simply providing a chronological narrative of "what" happened. Discussing multiple, specific actions allows candidates to provide a much more detailed explanation of why individuals or groups responded the way they did.

ACHIEVED

Identify & Describe

Candidates successfully described two perspectives and their corresponding actions or responses using relevant historical evidence.

  • Identify: Name the perspective clearly (e.g., The US Administration).
  • Describe Action: What did they actually do? (e.g., Dropped the atomic bombs).
  • Provide Evidence: Include a basic fact, quote, or date.
MERIT

Explain & Compare

Candidates explained the perspectives and actions, detailed the differences between them, and supported this with relevant evidence.

  • Explain Motivations: Why did they take this action? Focus on deep beliefs over simple narrative.
  • Compare: Explicitly state how and why Perspective A is fundamentally different from Perspective B.
  • Use Specific Evidence: Quote historical figures, use exact statistics, and integrate them into your sentences.
🏆
EXCELLENCE

Examine & Connect

Candidates discussed the perspectives and actions, deeply analyzed how or why they differed, and incorporated the wider historical context to fully develop their explanation.

  • Wider Context: Connect their actions to deeper motives or broader global shifts (e.g., The beginning of the Cold War, or the Meiji military tradition).
  • Evidence Leads: Your entire argument is built around complex evidence. Evidence proves the point, rather than just being tacked on.
  • Insightful Examination: Show deep understanding of the complexity and nuances of their beliefs.

Perspective Explorer

Compare the core values, beliefs, and motivations of the two sides.

🇺🇸 The US Administration

Truman, Stimson, Byrnes & Allies

Core Value / Belief

Democracy & Security: Rooted in Roosevelt's "Four Freedoms" (speech, worship, want, fear). They believed authoritarian militarism had to be eradicated globally to prevent future wars.

Primary Motivation

End the war swiftly & avoid invasion: Truman wanted to save an estimated 1 Million+ US casualties (Operation Downfall). A secondary motivation was to rebuild Japan as a strong, democratic, capitalist ally.

Historical Actions

  • Issued the Potsdam Declaration (July 26).
  • Dropped the atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.
  • Drafted the ambiguous "Byrnes Note" (Aug 11).

🇯🇵 Emperor Hirohito

The Japanese Leadership

Core Value / Belief

Kokutai (National Essence): The preservation of the traditional Imperial system and the Emperor's divine status. This was viewed as more important than military victory.

Primary Motivation

Prevent complete national collapse: Save Japan from total annihilation by atomic weapons and prevent a domestic communist revolution caused by famine and firebombing.

Historical Actions

  • Intervened to break the 3-3 "Big Six" government stalemate (Aug 9-10).
  • Delivered the "Jewel Voice Broadcast" announcing surrender directly to the public.

Primary Sources

Read and quote from the exact historical documents that shaped August 1945.

The Potsdam Declaration

July 26, 1945

"(1) We-the President of the United States, the President of the National Government of the Republic of China, and the Prime Minister of Great Britain... have conferred and agree that Japan shall be given an opportunity to end this war."

"(3) The result of the futile and senseless German resistance to the might of the aroused free peoples of the world stands forth in awful clarity as an example to the people of Japan... The full application of our military power, backed by our resolve, will mean the inevitable and complete destruction of the Japanese armed forces and just as inevitably the utter devastation of the Japanese homeland."

"(13) We call upon the government of Japan to proclaim now the unconditional surrender of all Japanese armed forces, and to provide proper and adequate assurances of their good faith in such action. The alternative for Japan is prompt and utter destruction."

The Jewel Voice Broadcast (Gyokuon-hōsō)

August 15, 1945

"To our good and loyal subjects: After pondering deeply the general trends of the world and the actual conditions obtaining in our empire today, we have decided to effect a settlement of the present situation by resorting to an extraordinary measure... We have ordered our empire to communicate to the governments of the United States, Great Britain, China and the Soviet Union that our empire accepts the provisions of their joint declaration."

"Moreover, the enemy has begun to employ a new and most cruel bomb, the power of which to do damage is, indeed, incalculable, taking the toll of many innocent lives. Should we continue to fight, not only would it result in an ultimate collapse and obliteration of the Japanese nation, but also it would lead to the total extinction of human civilization."

"We have resolved to pave the way for a grand peace for all the generations to come by enduring the unendurable and suffering what is insufferable."

Emperor Hirohito Emperor of Japan. Stepped in to break a 3-3 deadlock, prioritizing the survival of the Japanese nation over the military's desire for a final defense.
Harry S. Truman US President who authorized the atomic bombs to end the war swiftly.
Henry L. Stimson US Secretary of War. Advocated for allowing Japan to keep the Emperor to secure a prompt surrender.
James F. Byrnes US Secretary of State. Drafted the "Byrnes Note" keeping the Emperor's authority subject to the Supreme Commander.

Kantaro Suzuki Prime Minister of Japan. Asked the Emperor to make the final decision when the council was deadlocked.
Gen. Korechika Anami Japanese Minister of War. Hardliner pushing for Ketsu-Gō (decisive mainland battle).

Excellence Blueprint

How to structure your essay and include the Wider Context to get an 'E'.

The Secret to Excellence: Wider Context The overarching historical landscape (e.g., the impending Cold War, or the 70-year tradition of Meiji military dominance). Connecting to this shows deep understanding.

For the US: The Cold War

Truman wasn't just ending WWII; he was preparing for the Cold War. The occupation aimed to turn Japan into a democratic, capitalist shield against the Soviet Union in the Pacific.

Link: Why drop the bombs instead of invading? To end the war before the Soviets could claim territory in Japan.

For Hirohito: Meiji Tradition

By personally intervening to surrender, Hirohito broke decades of military dominance dating back to the 1868 Meiji Restoration. He sacrificed military pride to save the ultimate prize: the Kokutai.

Link: Why was the 3-3 deadlock such a big deal? Because Emperors traditionally never intervened in politics.

Paragraph Planners

1 The US Administration

Topic Sent.
Driven by the immediate desire to avoid massive casualties and the strategic goal of securing a post-war democratic ally, the US Administration’s perspective was anchored in achieving unconditional surrender.
Evidence
"Unconditional Surrender," Potsdam Declaration (July 26), 1M+ projected casualties (Operation Downfall), Truman's "rain of ruin", the Byrnes Note.
Excellence Link
Explain how the atomic bombs were not just a military strategy to end WWII, but a geopolitical move to assert US dominance over the Soviet Union (who invaded Manchuria on Aug 8) ahead of the Cold War.

2 Emperor Hirohito

Topic Sent.
Conversely, Emperor Hirohito’s perspective was defined by a desperate need to preserve the Kokutai (traditional Japanese imperial system) and prevent domestic revolution in the face of total destruction.
Evidence
Kokutai, breaking the 3-3 "Big Six" stalemate (Aug 9-10), "annihilation of the Japanese people", the "Jewel Voice Broadcast" (Aug 15).
Excellence Link
Explain how the dual shock of the atomic bombs and the Soviet invasion provided the external justification Hirohito needed to break decades of Meiji-era military dominance and force a surrender.

Not Achieved

The Americans dropped the atomic bomb on Japan in 1945. They did this because they wanted to win the war quickly. Truman was the president. After the bomb, Japan surrendered and then General MacArthur came to Japan to fix everything up.

Why is this NA? This is just storytelling. It states facts (Truman, 1945) but does not actually identify the perspective (values/beliefs) of the US. It just describes an event. It ignores the Chief Marker's advice to focus on the "why".
Achieved

The US Administration's perspective was that they needed to defeat Japan completely using "Unconditional Surrender". President Truman decided to drop the atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945. This action demonstrated their perspective because they believed a land invasion would cause too many casualties, so they used the bomb to force Japan to give up quickly and allow the US to rebuild the country as a democracy.

Why is this Achieved? It clearly identifies the perspective ("needed to defeat Japan completely") and describes the action taken to demonstrate it (dropping the bombs). It includes basic evidence ("Unconditional Surrender", Aug 1945).
Merit

The US Administration’s perspective was anchored in achieving "Unconditional Surrender" to permanently eradicate Japanese militarism. Driven by the motivation to avoid an estimated 1 million+ US casualties that a land invasion (Operation Downfall) could cause, President Truman authorized the use of atomic weapons in August 1945. This perspective differed significantly from Emperor Hirohito's. While the US prioritized securing a total military victory and establishing a post-war democracy, Hirohito's primary motivation was to preserve the Kokutai (National Essence) and his own divine status. This difference explains why the US demanded unconditional surrender, whereas Japan's leadership delayed, hoping to negotiate terms that would protect the Emperor.

Why is this Merit? It deeply explains the motivations (avoiding 1M+ casualties vs. preserving Kokutai). Crucially, it explicitly compares the two perspectives and explains why they differed. Uses excellent specific evidence.
🏆
Excellence

The US Administration’s perspective was anchored in achieving "Unconditional Surrender" to permanently eradicate Japanese militarism. Driven by the immediate motivation to avoid an estimated 1 million+ US casualties, President Truman authorized the use of atomic weapons. However, examining the wider historical context reveals a secondary, strategic motivation: the looming Cold War. Truman's actions demonstrated a perspective focused on asserting US dominance over the Soviet Union (who shattered Japan's peace hopes by invading Manchuria on August 8). By forcing a swift surrender and drafting the ambiguous "Byrnes Note" to keep the Emperor subordinate, the US perspective was to rapidly rebuild Japan into a capitalist, democratic bulwark against Communism in the Pacific, fundamentally differing from Japan's inward-looking focus on imperial survival.

Why is this Excellence? It does everything Merit does, but seamlessly connects the immediate motivations to the Wider Context (The Cold War, Soviet invasion on Aug 8, capitalist bulwark). The evidence (Byrnes Note) leads the complex explanation.
